Ontology vs Epistemology in Research
CP650 - Lec 10 2
• Ontology: addresses "what is"
• Epistemology: addresses "how do we know“
• Ontology: deals with reality of entities
• Epistemology: deals with methods of acquiring knowledge
• Ontology: concerned with categorizing entities
• Epistemology: concerned with evaluating evidence

Ontology vs Epistemology in Research
CP650 - Lec 10 3
• Ontology in UX  research: determining if a website is
considered a single entity or multiple entities
• Epistemology in UX  research: determining if usability testing 
is the most effective method for evaluating user satisfaction 
with a website
• Ontology in UX  research: determining if chatbot is 
considered as human or machine
• Epistemology in UX  research: determining if A/B testing is an 
appropriate method for evaluating the effectiveness of a 
chatbot's conversational design

Reliabilityin Research (2)
CP650 - Lec 10 8
• Reliability: consistency of research findings
• Repeatability of results under same conditions
• Reduces random errors, chance influences
• Two types: test-retest, inter-rater
• Test-retest: consistency over time on same individuals
• Inter-rater: consistency of results among multiple judges
• Ensures accuracy and trustworthiness of findings.

Validity in Research
CP650 - Lec 10 9
• Validity: accuracy of research findings
• Ensures conclusions are accurate and meaningful
• Ensures findings can be replicated, generalizable
• Two types: internal and external
• Internal: findings accurately reflect phenomenon
• External: findings generalizable to other settings/populations
• Important for reliability and trustworthiness of findings.

Internal Validity for UX  Studies
CP650 - Lec 10 10
• Study compares two sites (A and B)
• Participants shown site A first, then site B
• Same tasks completed on both sites
• This study design may not produce accurate results
• May not accurately identify the better design

Internal Validity for UX  Studies
CP650 - Lec 10 11
• Study setup favors design B
• Participants already familiar with testing situation and task 
domain
• May have expectations and know what's expected of them
• Lacks internal validity
• Solution is to alternate which site goes first and have half of 
the users try site B first

External Validity for UX  Studies
CP650 - Lec 10 12
A studyhasexternalvalidityiftheparticipantsandthestudy 
setuparerepresentativefor thereal-world situationin which 
thedesignis used but: 
• Recruiting study participants from the general population
may not be valid for seniors
• Testing mobile design on desktop may not generalize to real- 
world use
• Studies may lack external validity
• Further study may be required to confirm results.

Recommendations for Study Design: Internal Validity
CP650 - Lec 10 13
• Randomize task order to avoid bias
• People take longer and make more errors on first tasks
• Tasks at end of session may show effect of fatigue
• Use warmup tasks to mitigate learning phase and make 
participants comfortable
• Ensure warmup tasks are not included in analysis
• Sometimes, task order may not be feasible to randomize

Recommendations for Study Design: External Validity
CP650 - Lec 10 14
• Recruit representative participants matching target audience
demographics and goals
• External validity may not be ensured by demographics alone
• Strive to find participants with similar goals as users
• Replicate natural usage environment for best results
• Environment plays role in usability, test in location it will be 
used.

Reactivity in Research
CP650 - Lec 10 15
• Reactivity refers to change in behavior due to observation
• Can occur in quantitative and qualitative research
• Examples include Hawthorne effect, conformity effect, 
and social desirability bias
• Can affect research validity and generalizability.

Reactivity in Research: Hawthorn Effect
CP650 - Lec 10 16
• Hawthorne effect: phenomenon where participants change
behavior/performance due to increased attention in study
• Named after experiments at Hawthorne Works in 
1920s/1930s
• Can lead to inaccurate results if not controlled for in study
design.

Reactivity in Research: Conformity Effect
CP650 - Lec 10 17
• Conformity effect refers to the tendency for individuals to 
conform to the actions or opinions of a group, in order to fit 
in or be liked by others.
• It can occur in both quantitative and qualitative research.
• Examples include the Asch conformity experiments, where 
participants conformed to the incorrect answer given by a 
group, and groupthink, where individuals conform to group 
decision making without critically evaluating the decision.
• Conformity effect can lead to inaccurate research results if
not controlled for in the study design.

Reactivity in Research: Conformity Effect
CP650 - Lec 10 19
• Blind study design: Participants and/or researchers do not 
know the group assignments (control or experimental 
group)
• Double-blind study design: Both Participants and researchers 
do not know the group assignments.
• Helps to control for observer expectancy effect by reducing 
researcher bias.
• Commonly used in clinical trials to control for placebo 
effect.

Reactivity in Research: Social Desirability Effect
CP650 - Lec 10 20
• Social desirability effect: phenomenon where participants give 
responses that they believe are socially acceptable or 
desirable, rather than their true beliefs or behavior.
• Can occur in both quantitative and qualitative research.
• Examples include participants over-reporting positive
behaviors or under-reporting negative behaviors.
• Can lead to inaccurate research results if not controlled for 
in the study design.
• Anonymous surveys or indirect questioning methods can
help control for this effect.

Sampling
CP650 - Lec 10 22
• Sampling: selecting a subset of a population to study
• Representativeness: ensuring sample is representative of 
population
• Sample size: determining appropriate number of individuals 
to include
• Sampling bias: potential for sample to not accurately 
represent population

Sampling
CP650 - Lec 10 23
• Quant research: sample characteristics, size more important 
than relative size
• Qual research: not statistical representativeness, but full 
range of views, themes and possibilities.

Sampling

Sampling Categories
CP650 - Lec 10 25

• Non-probability sampling: non-random selection of

• Convenience sampling: selecting individuals easily accessible

recruiting participants through referrals

Ethics in Qualitative Research
CP650 - Lec 10 31
• Validity and reliability depend on investigator's ethics
• Ethical practice based on individual researcher's values and 
ethics
• Protection of subjects, informed consent, privacy, and deception 
must be considered
• Credibility of researcher, rigorous methods, and understanding 
of qualitative inquiry essential for credibility of qualitative 
research
• Credibility includes: training, experience, track record, status, self- 
presentation, intellectual rigor, professional integrity, and 
methodological competence
• Trust in study's integrity and researcher's ethical stance necessary.

Risk
CP650 - Lec 10 34
• Research should minimize the potential for harm to
participants
• In medical research, harm is often clear
• In user research, it can be more difficult to anticipate harm
• Identities of research participants are often undisclosed to 
protect against potential harm
• Research can unintentionally do harm through disclosures 
made during the research process
• It is important to consider potential harm when conducting 
research.

Privacy and Confidentiality
CP650 - Lec 10 35
• Privacy and confidentiality are important considerations in
research ethics
• Researchers should ensure that research participants cannot 
be identified from their responses
• This is important for both published and stored data
• Numeric codes or aliases may be used to protect 
participants' identities
• The more personal or "human" the research, the higher the 
risk of participants being identified by their responses
• Researchers should consider the potential for unintended 
harm through disclosures during research

Consent
CP650 - Lec 10 36
• Research involving human participants must follow the
principle of informed consent
• Participants must be fully aware of potential risks and 
understand the extent of those risks
• Risks cannot be hidden or minimized, even if they are minor
• In research where participants may reasonably expect harm, 
the benefits must outweigh the risks
• Participants must fully understand the risks and agree to take 
them
