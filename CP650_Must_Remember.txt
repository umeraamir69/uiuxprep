LECTURE 1 — Interaction design & UX
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

WHAT TO DESIGN (3 Ws + optimization)
  • Who the users are
  • What activities are carried out
  • Where interaction happens
  → Optimize interactions to match users’ activities and needs.

DEFINITIONS (remember authors)
  • Interaction design (Sharp, Rogers, Preece): designing interactive products to
    support how people communicate and interact in everyday and working lives.
  • Winograd: design of spaces for human communication and interaction.

GOALS OF INTERACTION DESIGN (2 main bullets)
  • Develop usable products (usable = easy to learn, effective, enjoyable).
  • Involve users in the design process.

RELATED TERMS (umbrella): UI design, software design, user-centered design,
  product design, web design, experience design (UX) — all under inter 
ACADEMIC DISCIPLINES CONTRIBUTING TO ID — COUNT: 6
  1. Psychology
  2. Social sciences
  3. Computing sciences
  4. Engineering
  5. Ergonomics
  6. Informatics

DESIGN PRACTICES CONTRIBUTING TO ID — COUNT: 5
  1. Graphic design
  2. Product design
  3. Artist-design
  4. Industrial design
  5. Film industry

INTERDISCIPLINARY FIELDS THAT “DO” INTERACTION DESIGN — COUNT: 5
  1. HCI
  2. Ubiquitous computing
  3. Human factors
  4. Cognitive ergonomics
  5. Information systems

MULTIDISCIPLINARY TEAMS — 2 sides
  • Benefits: more ideas and designs
  • Disadvantages: harder to communicate and move designs forward

USER EXPERIENCE (ideas to remember)
  • How product behaves in real world + feelings (Garrett: every used product has UX).
  • Nielsen & Norman: all aspects of end-user interaction with company, services, products.
  • You cannot fully “design” a user experience — you design FOR user experience.

HASENZAHL (2010) UX MODEL — COUNT: 2 dimensions
  • Pragmatic: simple, practical, obvious for achieving goals
  • Hedonic: how stimulating the interaction is

CHARACTERISTICS OF INTERACTION DESIGN — COUNT: 3
  1. Users involved throughout development
  2. Usability + UX goals identified, documented, agreed at start
  3. Iteration through core activities

USABILITY GOALS — COUNT: 6
  1. Effective to use
  2. Efficient to use
  3. Safe to use
  4. Good utility
  5. Easy to learn
  6. Easy to remember how to use

COMPANIES (one phrase each)
  • Nielsen Norman Group: human-centered products and services
  • Cooper: goal-related design (research → product)
  • IDEO: products, services, environments; new ways to provide value

LECTURE 2 — Accessibility & design principles
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

ACCESSIBILITY: extent product is usable by as many people as possible
  (slide focus: disabilities, e.g. VoiceOver / TalkBack).

INCLUSIVENESS: accommodate widest possible people (disability, education, age, income).

DISABILITY TYPES — COUNT: 3 main classes
  1. Sensory (vision, hearing, …)
  2. Physical (e.g. after stroke, spinal injury)
  3. Cognitive (learning, memory, aging)
  → Each type can vary by capability (e.g. peripheral vision, color blindness).

IMPAIRMENT TIMING — COUNT: 3
  1. Permanent
  2. Temporary (after accident/illness)
  3. Situational (e.g. noisy room → can’t hear)

DESIGN PRINCIPLES (core set from slides)
  • Visibility — see state + possible actions
  • Feedback — sound, highlight, animation, …
  • Constraints — limit wrong actions
  • Mapping — controls match layout/meaning (e.g. color-coded ports)
  • Consistency — similar operations/elements for similar tasks
  • Affordance (Norman) — attribute suggesting how to use object
  • Perceived affordances — for UIs: learned conventions, not “physical” affordances
  • Simplicity (Nielsen 1999) — remove what you can; balance with aesthetics

TYPES OF CONSISTENCY — COUNT: 3
  1. Aesthetic
  2. Internal (within one app)
  3. External (across apps/devices — often hard; e.g. phone vs calculator keypad)

LECTURE 3 — Process & Agile UX
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

WHAT ID INVOLVES (process)
  • Discover requirements → design → prototype → evaluate
  • Focus on users and goals; trade-offs; generate & choose alternatives

DESIGN APPROACHES — COUNT: 3 named
  1. User-centered design
  2. Activity-centered design
  3. Systems design
  (+ Double Diamond process link on slides)

PROBLEM SPACE — 3 explore questions
  • Current user experience?
  • Why change needed?
  • How will change improve situation?

INVOLVING USERS — 2 themes
  • Expectation management: realistic expectations, training, no hype
  • Ownership: users as stakeholders → more acceptance if problems

USER-CENTERED APPROACH — COUNT: 3 pillars
  1. Early focus on users/tasks — study 4 characteristic types:
     cognitive, behavioral, anthropomorphic, attitudinal
  2. Empirical measurement — scenarios, manuals, simulations, prototypes
  3. Iterative design — fix problems from testing, test again

FOUR BASIC ACTIVITIES OF INTERACTION DESIGN (title on slide; aligns with textbook)
  Requirements → design alternatives → prototyping → evaluation
  (Slides illustrate with Google Design Sprints.)

PRACTICAL ISSUES — COUNT: 5 questions listed
  Who are users? Needs? How generate alternatives? Choose among them?
  Integrate ID with other lifecycle models?

USER TYPES (cited): 382 distinct user types for smartphone apps (Sha Zhao et al., 2016).

HOW TO CHOOSE AMONG ALTERNATIVES (topics)
  Technical feasibility; evaluation with users/peers; prototypes (behavior matters);
  A/B testing; quality thresholds differ by stakeholder; UX/usability goals → criteria.

AGILE — sprint length: short timeboxes, typically ONE TO THREE WEEKS.
  Methods named: XP, Scrum, Kanban.

AGILE + ID integration reasons — COUNT: 4
  Tight iterations; early/regular feedback; emergent requirements;
  balance flexibility and structure.

TECHNICAL / UX DEBT: short-term compromises → long-term problems; neglecting UX;
  many independent products.

AGILE UX: 3 practical areas
  User research; aligning work practices; documentation

PARALLEL TRACKS (Agile UX)
  Vision before dev; design ~1 iteration ahead (sometimes 2).

CHAPTER 13 PRACTICE TOPICS — COUNT: 4
  Agile UX; design patterns; open-source resources; tools for ID

TOOLS (examples of what they support)
  Creativity, sketching, simulation, brainstorming, library search, mind mapping,
  video capture; wireframes → high-fidelity prototypes with pattern libraries.

OPEN-SOURCE EXAMPLES: pattern libraries (e.g. python-patterns.guide), Bootstrap.

LECTURE 4 — Conceptualizing interaction design
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

ASSUMPTION vs CLAIM
  • Assumption: taken for granted, needs investigation
  • Claim: stated as true but still open to question

CONCEPTUAL MODEL (Johnson & Henderson)
  High-level description of how system is organized and operates.

BENEFITS OF CONCEPTUALIZING — COUNT: 3
  Orientation; open-mindedness; common ground (shared terms)

CONCEPTUAL MODEL COMPONENTS — COUNT: 4 areas
  1. Metaphors and analogies
  2. Concepts exposed through product
  3. Task–domain objects, attributes, operations
  4. Relationships and mappings between concepts

INTERACTION TYPES — COUNT: 5
  1. Instructing — commands/options (repetitive, efficient)
  2. Conversing — dialogue (chatbots, voice, help)
  3. Manipulating — direct manipulation of objects
  4. Exploring — move through virtual/physical space or data
  5. Responding — system initiates; user may respond (Lueg et al., 2018)

INTERFACE STYLES (Lecture 4 list) — COUNT: 10 on slide
  Command, speech, data-entry, form fill-in, query, graphical, web, pen,
  augmented reality, gesture

CONCEPTUAL KNOWLEDGE SOURCES — COUNT: 5
  Paradigms, visions, theories, models, frameworks

NEW PARADIGM EXAMPLES — COUNT: 4
  Ubiquitous, pervasive, wearable computing, IoT

THEORY helps with factors — COUNT: 3 kinds named
  Cognitive, social, affective

MODEL EXAMPLES CITED
  Norman Seven Stages of Action; Hassenzahl UX model

FRAMEWORK EXAMPLE: DiCoT (Furniss & Blandford, 2006) — team use of tech

NORMAN (1988) CONCEPTUAL MODEL FRAMEWORK — COUNT: 3 parts
  1. Designer’s model — how designer thinks system should work
  2. System image — what user sees via UI, manuals, help
  3. User’s model — how user thinks system works

LECTURE 5 — COGNITIVE ASPECTS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

COGNITION (examples): thinking, remembering, learning, deciding, seeing,
  reading, talking, writing, …

HIGH-LEVEL CLASSIFICATIONS — COUNT: 2 pairs
  • Experiential vs reflective cognition (Norman, 1993)
  • Fast vs slow thinking (Kahneman, 2011)

WHY COGNITION MATTERS FOR HCI — COUNT: 3 outcomes
  What users can/can’t do; explain problems; theories/tools for better products

COGNITIVE PROCESSES (main slide list) — COUNT: 5
  1. Attention
  2. Perception
  3. Memory
  4. Learning
  5. Reading, speaking, and listening

ATTENTION
  • Focused vs divided; design: salience, grouping, avoid clutter
  • Tullis (1987): same density (~31%) but spacing/grouping changed search time
    ~5.5 s vs ~3.2 s (hotel rate task)

DRIVING + PHONE
  • Handheld or hands-free: conversation still competes for attention — risky
  • Passenger can stop talking when hazard appears; remote caller often cannot

PERCEPTION DESIGN NOTES
  Legible text; distinguishable icons; borders/spacing group (Weller 2004)
  Yellow on black/blue OK; yellow on green/white poor

MEMORY
  • Recognition easier than recall; encoding + attention → remembering
  • Henkel (2014): photographed objects remembered less than observed live
  • Miller 7±2: caution — scanning menus ≠ holding all in working memory
  • PIM problem: many files — folders, naming, search, smart search

LEARNING TYPES — COUNT: 2
  • Incidental (e.g. recognize faces)
  • Intentional (studying, cooking) — harder; people prefer learning by doing

MODALITIES (slide comparison)
  Many prefer listening; reading can be faster; listening can be less effort than
  reading/speaking; dyslexia affects written words.

APPLICATION TYPES NAMED: VUIs (Siri, Alexa, Google Voice); TTS; chatbots / NL systems

LECTURE 6 — SOCIAL INTERACTION
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

CONVERSATION MECHANISMS: greetings, turn-taking, back-channel, farewells,
  repair (repeat/clarify).

SACKS ET AL. (1978) — THREE BASIC TURN-TAKING RULES
  Rule 1: Current speaker picks next (question, request, opinion)
  Rule 2: Another person self-selects to speak
  Rule 3: Current speaker continues

REMOTE CONVERSATION MEDIA (examples): email, IM, video, chatrooms

VIDEOWINDOW (Bellcore, 1989): ~3×8 ft video “window”; 50 miles apart;
  findings (Kraut, 1990): talked about system; more talk to co-located people;
  “lean in” broke AV pickup; hard to monitor

PRESENCE / COLLABORATION IDEAS
  • Social presence (e.g. VR social)
  • Telepresence robots (e.g. Beam+)
  • Co-presence: tabletops, whiteboards, shared spaces

COORDINATION (co-located groups) — COUNT: 3 means
  Verbal/non-verbal; schedules/rules/conventions; shared external representations

AWARENESS (Dourish & Bly, 1992 area)
  • Peripheral awareness — edge of attention, overhearing/overseeing
  • Situational awareness — understand environment to act (ATC, surgery)

REFLECTTABLE: LEDs showed how much each member spoke (microphones).

SOCIAL ENGAGEMENT: participation in group; voluntary exchange; hubs/retweets

ETHICS EXAMPLE: Eugenia Kuyda — chatbot from deceased friend’s texts

LECTURE 9 — DATA GATHERING
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

FIVE KEY ISSUES
  1. Setting goals (+ plan analysis before collecting)
  2. Identifying participants (+ how many)
  3. Relationship with participants (professional, informed consent)
  4. Triangulation (multiple perspectives / data types)
  5. Pilot studies (small trial first)

DATA CAPTURE: notes, audio, video, photos (alone or combined)

INTERVIEW TYPES
  Unstructured (rich, not replicable)
  Structured (scripted, replicable, less rich)
  Semi-structured (balance)
  Focus groups: usually 3–10 people; closed vs open questions

RUNNING AN INTERVIEW — COUNT: 5 phases
  Introduction → Warm-up → Main body → Cool-off → Closure

QUESTIONNAIRE RESPONSE FORMATS (examples)
  Yes/No, checkboxes, rating scales, Likert, semantic differential (3/5/7+ points),
  open-ended at end

RESPONSE RATES (slide): ~40% good; ~20% often acceptable

USER STORY FORMAT (Agile)
  As a <role>, I want <behavior> so that <benefit>

REQUIREMENT KINDS (summary slide) — COUNT: 6 areas
  1. Functional — what system does
  2. Data — what to store, how
  3. Environment / context: physical, social, organizational, technical
  4. Users — who; novice/expert/frequent/casual patterns
  5. Usability goals
  6. User experience goals

SEVEN PRODUCT DIMENSIONS — Gottesdiener & Gorman (2012) [title on slide; memorize name]

DATA GATHERING FOR REQUIREMENTS
  Interviews, observation, questionnaires; often combine; documentation; similar products

PROBE TYPES (examples): cultural probe; design probe; technology/provocative probe

CONTEXTUAL INQUIRY
  • Interviews ~1.5–2 h; master–apprentice model
  • FOUR PRINCIPLES: Context; Partnership; Interpretation; Focus
  • “Cool concepts”: joy of life vs joy of use (subtopics under each)

BRINGING REQUIREMENTS TO LIFE
  Personas (typical synthesized users); scenarios (stories); use cases

USE CASE STYLES — COUNT: 2
  Essential (intention/responsibility, no implementation detail)
  Normal + alternative courses (more detailed)
